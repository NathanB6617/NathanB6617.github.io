A Future Scientist

Hello! My name is Nathan Burns, a Physics and Applied Mathematics student at Yale University with research experience in high-energy nuclear collider development and research. As a member of the Electron-Ion Collider (EIC) collaboration under the mentorship of Dr. Helen Caines, I contribute to the development, characterization, and testing of detector systems for next-generation nuclear physics experiments.

My work spans both software and hardware, including analysis of experimental data, instrumentation, component testing, control systems (GUIs), and scientific software development. I enjoy tackling complicated technical problems that cross between physics, engineering, and computing. Beyond research, I develop video games with Amorium Labs, work on a liquid-powered rocket team, and perform and choreograph ballet for Yale Ballet Company.

I am particularly interested in particle and nuclear physics, astrophysics, fusion energy, space exploration, and advancing technologies that expand humanity's scientific capabilities. Whether working on a research detector, developing software, or designing new systems, I am driven by a desire to contribute to discoveries that deepen our understanding of the universe and improve the world around us.

Research on the ePIC Detector for the EIC

The Electron-Ion Collider (EIC) is currently under R&D and construction at Brookhaven National Laboratory. The EIC currently has one major detector at IR6 - the Electron-Proton-Ion Collider (ePIC) detector. One of the subdetectors in the forward-going position is the Longitudinally Segmented Forward Hadronic Calorimeter (LFHCal), made up of a SiPM-on-Tile design. This research aims to perform quality testing on multiple components and ensure success for future developments in the detector.

YUAA Project Liquid

Project Liquid is a part of the Yale Undergraduate Aerospace Association and hopes to achieve rocket flight via liquid-powered fuel. This team is new but has already had their first cold flow and static fire test of their first complete engine for their rocket: Veritas. I am currently a member of the Structures subteam and have led the group during testing, installing ground stakes, and machining multiple parts for the rocket.

Amorium Labs Game Development

Amorium Labs is Yale's only undergraduate game development team, developing complicated games in Unity since 2019. These games provide an outlet of expression for students, while also offering a place where one can learn and improve their skills in coding, story development, team leadership, and more.
