Overview

During the STARS Summer Research Program at Yale University's Wright Laboratory, I worked with researchers contributing to the ePIC detector, one of the major experiments being developed for the future Electron-Ion Collider (EIC). My research focused on detector technologies used in modern calorimeters, specifically silicon photomultipliers (SiPMs), scintillating tiles, and detector characterization through radioactive source testing. Through this work I gained experience with detector assembly, experimental testing, data analysis, and the physics underlying particle detector systems.

Silicon Photomultipliers (SiPMs)

Silicon photomultipliers are semiconductor photon detectors capable of detecting extremely small amounts of light. Their compact size, low operating voltage, and high sensitivity make them attractive candidates for modern calorimeter systems.

My work involved studying detector response, signal characteristics, and operational performance of SiPMs coupled to scintillating materials. This research helped evaluate detector technologies being considered for implementation within the ePIC calorimeter system.

Scintillating Tiles

Scintillating tiles form the active detection medium in many sampling calorimeters. When charged particles pass through the material, light is produced and subsequently collected by photodetectors such as SiPMs.

I worked with scintillating tiles integrated directly with SiPMs, helping characterize detector performance and investigate how different detector configurations affected signal quality and light collection efficiency.

Radiation and Light Yield Studies

Detector characterization was performed using radioactive sources to generate measurable signals within scintillating materials.

By analyzing waveform and charge distributions, detector light yield could be quantified and compared across different detector designs. These measurements provided important insight into detector performance and helped validate SiPM-on-tile technologies for future calorimeter applications.
